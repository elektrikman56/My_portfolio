This interdisciplinary research treats the process of burglary as a dynamic, controllable system rather than a random event. By applying Data Mining techniques, a comprehensive dataset was constructed from over 100 real-world-based experimental samples, incorporating more than 20 distinct variables. The project demonstrates the power of Linear Regression and Control Theory in quantifying human behavior and optimizing complex systems.

The Dataset: 100+ Samples & Multidimensional Variables

The strength of this model lies in its diverse data sources. To achieve high predictive accuracy, the following parameters were meticulously analyzed for each of the 100+ cases:

  • Offender Profile: Age, years of experience, and whether they acted according to a pre-defined plan.
  • Property Characteristics: House type (detached, apartment), presence of security systems (alarms, cameras), and the type of locks used.
  • Environmental Factors: Location of the property, presence of witnesses, and the specific time of the incident.
  • Victim Profile: Income level and homeowner habits.

Mathematical Modeling & Compensator Design

Using MATLAB, the data was processed through supervised learning models to predict two primary outputs: Financial Loss (USD) and Operation Duration (Minutes). To further "perfect" the efficiency of this system, I designed classical control compensators:

  • Linear Regression: Used to establish the baseline relationship between input variables (like experience and planning) and the resulting outputs.
  • Lead Compensator: Designed to increase the system's response speed, analyzing how time efficiency is optimized within the criminal process.
